Behind on Your Mortgage in Hawaiʻi? Here's What to Do Next

Loan & Mortgage Foreclosure By Gift Kitkuakul, Ph.D. · July 2, 2026

If you've missed a mortgage payment — or you can see that the next one isn't going to happen either — I want you to know something most homeowners on Oʻahu don't: you almost certainly have more time and more options than you think.

The fear kicks in fast. People stop opening mail. They avoid calls from numbers they don't recognize. They assume the worst is already decided. But in most cases, being behind on your mortgage is a beginning — not an ending — and what you do in the next few weeks matters far more than the missed payment itself.

What actually happens when you miss a payment?

Missing one payment doesn't trigger foreclosure. What it typically triggers is a late fee and a note on your credit report. Most mortgage servicers are required to make contact and offer loss mitigation information before they can legally begin foreclosure proceedings — and in Hawaiʻi, that formal process generally doesn't begin until a homeowner is 90 to 120 days delinquent.

That window — those first three to four months — is the most important time to act. Not because foreclosure is imminent, but because that's when the most options are still available.

What options might still be on the table?

The answer depends on your lender, your loan type, how far behind you are, and your financial situation. But here's what many Oʻahu homeowners don't realize is possible:

  • Loan modification — a permanent change to your loan terms (lower rate, extended term, or rolled-in arrears) to make your monthly payment manageable. Learn more about loan modification in Hawaiʻi →
  • Repayment plan — catching up on missed payments gradually, spread over several months, while continuing your regular payment.
  • Forbearance — a temporary pause or reduction in payments while you recover from a hardship like job loss, medical bills, or a family situation.
  • Reinstatement — paying the total amount owed in one lump sum to bring the loan current. This is feasible for some homeowners with available funds or family support.
  • Short sale — if keeping the home isn't the goal, selling for less than what's owed (with lender approval) may be a better outcome than foreclosure. Understand the difference between a short sale and foreclosure →
  • Selling before foreclosure begins — if there's equity in the home, selling on your terms preserves more of what you've built than waiting for a lender to force the sale.

The earlier you explore these options, the more of them remain available. Most homeowners who lose options don't lose them because the situation was unsolvable — they lose them because they waited too long to find out what was possible.

What if you've already received a notice?

If you've gotten a formal notice in the mail — whether it says "Notice of Default," "Notice of Intention to Foreclose," or something similar — the options above may still be available to you. A notice is not a sale. It's a legal step in a process that, in Hawaiʻi, still takes months to complete.

What you do NOT want to do is ignore it. Each notice has specific timelines attached to it. Understanding what you received — and what it means for your timeline — is the critical first step. Read: What to do when you receive a foreclosure notice in Hawaiʻi →

Why Oʻahu is different

Hawaiʻi's housing market and legal environment create unique pressures and unique opportunities for homeowners. Property values on Oʻahu remain among the highest in the country, which means many homeowners who are behind on payments actually have significant equity — equity that can be protected with the right information and the right timing.

At the same time, the cost of living makes it harder to recover from financial setbacks, and many homeowners don't know that resources specific to Hawaiʻi — like the Hawaii HomeOwnership Center — offer free HUD-approved housing counseling tailored to local situations.
